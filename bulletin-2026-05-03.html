THE MASS – THE PREFACE

Continuing to consider the Preface of the Mass [the prayer which introduced the Holy

Holy and the Eucharistic Prayer], following the opening dialogue, which we have

already considered, we enter into the substance of the Preface itself. The Preface

begins with the following address to God the Father: It is truly right and just, our duty

and our salvation, always and everywhere to give you thanks, Lord, holy Father,

almighty and eternal God, through Christ our Lord. It takes up the final part of the

introductory dialogue – “It is right and just” [to lift up our hearts to the Lord],

whereby the Celebrant takes up the acclamation of the people, joining themselves to

the uprightness and holiness that belongs to God himself. The prayer continues with

the affirmation that it is “our duty and our salvation always and everywhere to give

you thanks”. This powerful statement of faith bears reflection. The act of

thanksgiving is not, for the Christian, a bland sentiment of appreciation: it is both a

duty and – extraordinarily- our salvation! We have already considered how the very

name of the Mass – the Holy Eucharist – means Thanksgiving, understood as Christ’s

own act of praise to God offered on the cross as an offering ‘breath unto fragrance as he offered it to God’ [cf. Ephesians 5:2]. It is in this sense that thanksgiving is a

duty and our very salvation. It is a duty of the soul that believes and acknowledges

God, and specifically acknowledges the full mystery of God revealed in Jesus Christ.

For this reason it is also our salvation to acknowledge God’s wonderful work in Jesus

Christ. It is both an act of faith in Jesus Christ and a joining of ourselves to his

sacrificial action on the cross and in the resurrection, which is made manifest in the

sacrifice of the Mass. Christ is acknowledged as the ‘firstborn of all creation’, the

‘eternal Son of the Father’, the one through whom all creation came to be and has

been redeemed, the New Man, the Second Adam, the ‘firstborn from the dead’, the

one through whom all sin is forgiven and souls are made new, reborn in baptism to be

sons and daughters of God the Father. The believing Christian is, therefore, bound to

acknowledge God’s work in Jesus Christ the co-eternal Son, and to acknowledge all

that he has made and re-made; to acknowledge his work in creation and his mission

of love and redemption from the beginning to this very day and to the end of time.

Thanksgiving, therefore, is not a mere ‘nod of the head’ nor a platitude expressed by

familiar formulas: it is the very response of the soul seized by the mystery of God’s

loving action from the beginning of creation through the work of redemption in Jesus

Christ and His providential direction of all history and existence to his own all-

consuming love. It is above all our part in Christ’s own self-offering to the Father.

**THE SACRAMENT OF CONFIRMATION**

The Sacrament of Confirmation in the Catholic Church is a primary agreement with the working of the Holy Spirit that deepens and increases the grace received via Baptism. Its primary effect is to give the confirmed a special strength of the Holy Spirit to spread and defend the faith by word and action, enabling them to be courageous witnesses for Christ and never be ashamed of the Cross (CCC 1303). This sacrament completes the grace of the universal priesthood of the faithful, authorising the confirmed to evangelise and actively witness their faith for Christ and others (CCC 1305). Like Baptism, Confirmation imprints an indelible spiritual mark on the soul and thus may only be received once.

Who can receive Confirmation? Any baptised person who has reached the age of reason and spiritual maturity (typically around six or seven years old) may be confirmed. Proper instruction and preparation are required, which involve not only doctrinal understanding of the faith and sacraments but also fostering a deeper relationship with Christ through prayer and attentiveness to the gifts of the Holy Spirit. Confirmation candidates should be in a state of grace, and it is recommended they receive the sacrament of Reconciliation beforehand. They also select a sponsor, usually one of their baptismal sponsors, reinforcing the unity between Baptism and Confirmation.

The ordinary minister of Confirmation in the Latin Rite is the bishop, signifying the visible link between the expanded Church and the strengthening of the Church under the bishop’s leadership. However, the bishop may delegate a priest to confer Confirmation, especially during the Easter Vigil or other special occasions. In cases of danger of death, any priest may administer Confirmation to a baptised but unconfirmed Catholic.

The rites itself consist of four parts: (1) candidates renew their baptismal promises and profess their faith; (2) a prayer is recited for the gifts of the Spirit, for the entire group; (3) the bishop lays hands on each candidate and anoints their forehead with Chrism while saying, “Be sealed with the Gift of the Holy Spirit.” By this act, they are anointed and enrolled in His service forever (CCC 1296); (4) a sign of peace marks the ceremony, expressing ecclesial communion.

Through Confirmation, believers transition from the Church’s fold of Baptism to living by an outward expression of faith which solidifies their Christian maturity. With their faith and baptismal grace thus deepened, they courageously bear witness to the Gospel.
