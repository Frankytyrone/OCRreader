**THE MASS – THE PREFACE**

Continuing to consider the Preface of the Mass (the prayer which introduced the Holy Holy and the Eucharistic Prayer), following the opening dialogue, which we have already considered, we enter into the substance of the Preface itself. The Preface begins with the following address to God the Father: It is truly right and just, our duty and our salvation, always and everywhere to give you thanks, Lord, holy Father, almighty and eternal God, through Christ our Lord. It takes up the final part of the introductory dialogue – “It is right and just” [to lift up our hearts to the Lord], whereby the Celebrant takes up the acclamation of the people, joining themselves to the uprightness and holiness that belongs to God himself. The prayer continues with the affirmation that it is “our duty and our salvation always and everywhere to give you thanks”. This powerful statement of faith bears reflection. The act of thanksgiving is not, for the Christian, a bland sentiment of appreciation: it is both a duty and – extraordinarily – our salvation! We have already considered how the very name of the Mass – the Holy Eucharist – means Thanksgiving, understood as Christ’s own act of praise to God offered on the cross as an offering ‘breath;out fragrance as he offered it to God’ [cf. Ephesians 5:2]. It is in this sense that thanksgiving is a duty and our very salvation. It is a duty of the soul that believes and acknowledges God, and specifically acknowledges the fully mystery of God revealed in Jesus Christ. For this reason it is also our salvation to acknowledge God’s wonderful work in Jesus Christ. It is both an act of faith in Jesus Christ and a joining of ourselves to his salvific action on the cross and in the resurrection, which is made manifest in the sacrifice of the Mass. Christ is acknowledged as the ‘firstborn of all creation’, the ‘eternal Son of the Father’, the one through whom all creation came to be and has been redeemed, the New Man, the Second Adam, the ‘firstborn from the dead’, the one through whom all sin is forgiven and souls are made new, reborn in baptism to be sons and daughters of God the Father. The believing Christian is, therefore, bound to acknowledge God’s work in Jesus Christ the co-eternal Son, and to acknowledge all that he has made and re-made; to acknowledge his work in creation and his mission of love and redemption from the beginning to this very day and the end of time. Thanksgiving, therefore, is not a mere ‘nod of the head’ nor a platitude expressed by familiar formulas: it is the very response of the soul seized by the mystery of God’s loving action from the beginning of creation through the work of redemption in Jesus Christ and His provident direction of all history and existence to his own all- consuming love. It is above all our part in Christ’s own self-offering to the Father.

**THE SACRAMENT OF CONFIRMATION** in the Catholic Church is a pivotal sacrament of initiation involving the Holy Spirit that deepens and completes the grace received at baptism. Its primary effect is to give the confirmed a special strength of the Holy Spirit to spread and defend the faith by word and action, enabling them to be courageous witnesses of Christ and to increase the bond of faith with the Church (CCC 1303).

This sacrament emphasises the role of the universal Priesthood of the faithful, authorising the confirmed to evangelise and actively witness their faith (force of her office) (CCC 1305). Like baptism, Confirmation imprints an indelible spiritual mark on the soul and thus may only be received once.

Who can receive Confirmation? Any baptised person who has reached the age of reason and spiritual maturity (typically around six or seven years old) may be confirmed. Proper instruction and preparation are required, which involve not only doctrinal understanding of the faith and sacraments but also fostering a deeper relationship with Christ through prayer and attentiveness to the gifts of the Holy Spirit. Confirmation candidates should be in a state of grace, and it is recommended they receive the sacrament of Reconciliation beforehand. They also select a sponsor, typically one of their baptismal sponsors, reinforcing the unity between Baptism and Confirmation.

The ordinary minister of Confirmation in the Latin Rite is the bishop, signifying the link between the sacrament's graces and the strengthening of the Church under the bishop's leadership. However, the bishop may delegate a priest to confer Confirmation, especially during the Easter Vigil or other special occasions. In cases of danger of death, any priest may administer Confirmation to a baptised but unconfirmed person.

The rite itself consists of four parts: (1) candidates renew their baptismal promises and profess their faith; (2) a prayer invokes the Holy Spirit to come down upon the group; (3) the bishop lays hands on each candidate and anoints their foreheads with chrism while saying, "Be sealed with the Gift of the Holy Spirit." She/he sees that the individual is enrolled in His service (CCC1296); (4) a sign of peace ends the ceremony.

Contrasting the Western tradition of separating Confirmation fundamentally from the Church's liturgical rites, it is common that the sacrament of Confirmation arises as infant Baptism became widespread, leading to a separation of the two. The Catholic Church in the East retained the imposition of hands (Confirmation) for older baptized individuals and exercising these gifts through the Holy Spirit uniformly for a sole ecclesiastical community. The Eastern Church, however, continues to administer Baptism and Confirmation together, even for infants.
