Rosary in the Month of May In this month of May, dedicated to Our Blessed Mother, we are invited to deepen our love for the Rosary. As Pope John Paul II said, “The Rosary is my favourite prayer... marvellous in its simplicity and its depth.” Pope Francis encourages us, “The Rosary is the prayer that always accompanies my life,” while Mother Teresa reminded us, “Cling to the Rosary as the creeper clings to the tree.” Saint John Vianney called it “a powerful weapon... to keep oneself from sin,” and Pope Pius IX urged, “Give me an army saying the Rosary and I will conquer the world.” With the wisdom of the saints and the guidance of the Church, may we take up this prayer daily, walking with Mary ever closer to Christ. In the quiet repetition of its prayers, we find space for grace to work within us and for our intentions to be gently lifted to God. May every bead draw us more deeply into the love of Christ and the tender care of His Mother.

Reflection

It seems strange to hear part of Jesus’ last supper discourse repeated during late Eastertime. It makes sense

only in its liturgical context: Jesus’ words at the time of his saying goodbye.

The disciples sound much like young children when parents tell them that they are going out. Where are you

going? When are you coming back? Who is going to stay with us? Did you notice that the disciples – like

young children - did not ask what is going to happen with Jesus; they ask only what is going to happen to

them. Jesus is not talking about where he is going as a place with an address; nor does he call it heaven.

Jesus is talking about relationships, his and our relationship with the father. Through faith, the disciples will be

able to recognize the relationship that already exists between Jesus and his father. Ultimately, faith, trust in

God, will allow his followers to enter fully into that divine relationship: mutual indwelling. That is “the way, the

life” in his truthfully proclaiming himself: “The way, the truth and the life.”

This same reality is what he calls elsewhere “The Kingdom of the Father.” The interpenetrating of the divine and our human consciousness is

“the belonging” that we all desire in the depths of our hearts. This is the heart of John’s message. A bit lofty? Absolutely! John is depicted as

an eagle in Christian art because of his lofty, theological soaring, not because he had feathers. Jesus assures his listeners that they who

believe in him will do the works he does—and even greater works. The list is long: caring for the sick, forgiving, comforting those in pain,

protecting the weak and vulnerable, embracing the poor, eating with sinners, defending the rights of the victimized, denouncing injustice . . .

and more.

THE SACRAMENT OF CONFIRMATION in the Catholic Church is a preeminent witness of the outpouring of the Holy Spirit that deepens and increases the grace received at Baptism. Its primary effect is to give the confirmed a special strength of the Holy Spirit to spread and defend the faith in word and action, enabling them to be courageous witnesses to Christ and to defend the name of Christ (CCC 1303). This sacrament completes the grace of the universal priesthood of the faithful, authorising the confirmed to evangelise and to actively witness their faith (The Catechism of office) (CCC 1305). Like Baptism, Confirmation imprints an indelible spiritual mark on the soul and thus may only be received once.

Who can receive Confirmation? Any baptised person who has reached the age of reason and spiritual maturity (typically around six or seven years old) may be confirmed. Proper instruction and preparation are required, which involve not only doctrinal understanding of the faith and sacraments but also fostering a deeper relationship with Christ through prayer and attentiveness to the gifts of the Holy Spirit. Confirmation candidates should be in a state of grace, and it is recommended they receive the sacrament of Reconciliation beforehand. They also select a sponsor, usually one of their baptismal sponsors, reinforcing the unity between Baptism and Confirmation.

The ordinary minister of Confirmation in the Latin Rite is the bishop, signifying the close link between the sacraments of grace and the strengthening of the Church under the bishop’s leadership. However, the bishop may delegate a priest to confer confirmation, especially during the Easter Vigil or other special occasions. In cases of danger of death, any priest may administer Confirmation to a baptised but uninfirmed person.

The rite itself consists of four parts: (1) candidates renew their baptismal promises and profess their faith; (2) a prayer of invocation for the Spirit is said over the group; (3) the bishop lays hands on each candidate and anoints their forehead with chrism while saying, “Be sealed with the Gift of the Holy Spirit.” (4) the ceremony ends with the newly confirmed enrolled in his/her service (CCC 1296); (4) a sign of peace marks the ceremony, expressing ecclesiastical communion between the confirmed and the congregation from the Church’s offerings. Simply baptising infants and the baptismally unsealed, the sacrament of Confirmation arose as infant Baptism became widespread, leading to new theological practices. The Archbishop imparts an individual laying of hands (Confirmation) for older baptised individuals means of exercising the gift of the Holy Spirit in adolescence and integrating them into the community. The Eastern Church, however, continues to administer Baptism and Confirmation together, even to infants.
