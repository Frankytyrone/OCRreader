## Be Nourished by the Eucharist

![img-2.jpeg](img-2.jpeg)

![img-3.jpeg](img-3.jpeg)

Bread of some kind is a staple in the diet of almost every human civilisation. Throughout both the Hebrew Bible and the New Testament, bread is used as a symbol of spiritual sustenance. Abraham offers bread to the three travellers, the triune God, as a mark of hospitality. The Hebrews eat unleavened bread before beginning their Exodus from slavery in Egypt. Following their mutiny in the desert, God feeds them with manna from heaven.

Jesus echoes Yahweh by feeding the five thousand on the hillside. Though they had sought him out in their spiritual hunger, he did not neglect their physical hunger. The disciples urged him to send the crowd to the nearby towns to get food out of their own pocket, but this was not Jesus’ style. Instead, he had them sit in groups and fed them on five loaves and two fish. They even gathered the leftover crumbs.

The bread of the Israelites and the bread eaten by those on the hillside are a precursor to the ultimate bread of the Last Supper. The Eucharist does not sate physical hunger or preserve physical life, but rather satisfies spiritual hunger, sustains spiritual life. This reality is not easily understood or accepted. In fact, one of the primary divisions within the Christian churches centres on the nature of the Eucharist. Though we attempt to offer parallels and interpretations, words become particularly weak at this point in our faith. And when words fail, we let actions speak.

So often in our efforts to comfort someone, to communicate our grief or our joy or to express our love for them, we don’t speak. We reach out and touch their hand, put our arms around them or kiss them. We do this because physical touch expresses what words cannot. The Eucharist is God’s physical touch. We hear his words, reflect on his miracles and seek to live his values, but nothing connects us with God the way receiving the Eucharist does. Its touch reveals more to us than words ever could.

## Thought for the Day

The Eucharist gives us the experience of rediscovering the 'Community', and of 'Encountering the community'. It is the laboratory of fraternity. This is why a Christian can never be content with personal prayer because there are times when the Community, Jesus's friends, gather together to pray. This is the Eucharist. In this gathering, we listen to the Word and nourish ourselves on the Eucharist.

Lord Jesus, in gathering your disciples around the table, you teach me that there is no Eucharist without the community, and there is no community without service. Help me to make my life a Eucharistic life.

—Fr. Andrea Vena

To mark the 125th Anniversary of the consecration of the Cathedral of Saints Eunan and Columba, Letterkenny, the Holy See has granted a Plenary Indulgence under the usual conditions. It may be obtained as follows: The Plenary Indulgence may be obtained under the usual conditions by any of the faithful who, rightly disposed, participate in the liturgical life of the Cathedral with the intention of gaining the Indulgence, beginning with the Mass of Christn on Holy Thursday of this year and concluding with the feast of the Epiphany, 6th January 2027. The same Indulgence may be obtained by the sick and others who, unable to leave their homes, participate spiritually in the same liturgical services by electronic means.

The indulgence may be gained under the usual conditions by all who piously come on pilgrimage to the Cathedral in the aforementioned period with that purpose in mind.

The same Indulgence is extended to those who wish to obtain it by spending at least one hour in Eucharistic Adoration at the St. Pius X Chapel of Perpetual Adoration in the grounds of the Cathedral.

To obtain an indulgence, the usual conditions of detachment from all sin, Sacramental Confession, Holy Communion, and prayer for the intentions of the Pope must be met within 7 days.

## Reflection

During his life on earth, Jesus’ favourite way of expressing his love and his desire to be one with people, especially for those who were rejected and unloved, was to share meals with them. Shared meals were, for the Jews, signs of acceptance and friendship. Like many of us, the Jews were rather selective about those with whom they shared meals. In seeking out sinners and tax collectors, Jesus was contravening their traditions. Most Jews invited only friends or powerful people to their meals. In eating with sinners, Jesus was making friends with those who had no friends. He was showing them respect and love. He was letting them see themselves in a new light and become a new people.

Instead of a people despised and rejected, they were God’s beloved children and citizens of his Kingdom. Jesus’ last meal with his disciples is inseparable from the sacrifice of his life on the Cross, his supreme act of love. Love is manifested supremely in self-sacrifice. In receiving the body and blood of Christ in the Eucharist, we become one, not only with Jesus, but with one another. Every Eucharist ends with a sending on Mission: ‘Go in Peace to love and serve the Lord’. We are commissioned to bring the message of the Eucharist to the world. Just as the Jesus has become our Food, giving himself completely to us, so we, too, must give ourselves for the sake of the world. Our celebration of the this great feast of Corpus Christi reminds us of our constant challenge: to keep alive the blessed memory of Jesus by becoming, in the context of our time, his flesh and blood given for the life of the world. So let us pray: ‘Lord, we thank you for the gift of your Body and Blood through which we are nourished and strengthened to share your love with our brothers and sisters. Amen’.

CORPUS CHRISTI is the feast instituted by Pope Urban IV on August 11, 1264, and celebrated on the Sunday after Trinity Sunday to honour Christ's real presence in the Eucharist and the fulfilment of His promise to be with us "even until the end of the age" (Mt. 28:20). The feast includes a Eucharistic procession, with the priest and faithful accompanying the exposed host in a monstrance, and it culminates in Eucharistic adoration. Urban IV also commissioned St. Thomas Aquinas to compose the hymns for the celebration, including "O Salutaris," "Tantum Ergo," and "Panis Angelicus."

Its deeper meaning is that the Eucharist is not a symbol only, but Christ Himself truly present—His whole person, body and blood—under the appearances of bread and wine. The sources connect this to Jesus 'words at the Last Supper, Saint Paul's teaching in 1 Corinthians, and John 6, and they present the feast as a public, joyful confession of the Church's faith in the Real Presence.

Historically, Corpus Christi grew within a broader renewal of Eucharistic devotion: adoration, exposition, procession, reservation of the Blessed Sacrament, and Benediction flourished in the thirteenth and fourteenth centuries, and later the Council of Trent reaffirmed and promoted Eucharistic reverence and procession.

In short, Corpus Christi is the Church's solemn celebration of the Eucharist as Christ's living gift of Himself—His unconditional love, saving sacrifice, and abiding presence among His people.
