## Alleluia Alleluia Alleluia Alleluia Alleluia

‘If you want somebody to understand what the major intellects of the world religions mean by God, you could do a great deal worse than introduce them to Anna.’

Archbishop of Canterbury Dr Rowan Williams is among the many fans of Mister God, This is Anna, the story of four-year-old Anna and her friendship with 16-year-old Fynn, the author. Anna’s outpourings on life, science and God, or ‘Mister God’ as she calls him, have entranced readers for years, bursting as they do with joy, inquisitiveness and insight. During a discussion on ‘Mister God’ and the ways in which he is different from people, Anna concludes: ‘So you see, Fynn, people can’t talk about Mister God from the outside; you can only talk about Mister God from the inside of him.’

As an illustration of the mystery of God’s presence with us, it goes a long way. It is this mystery that Jesus breaks down for his disciples in today’s Gospel when he promises to send his Spirit to be with them: ‘On that day you will understand that I am in my Father and you in me and I in you’. Four-year-old Anna seems to have a handle on this mystery.

Thoughts on the Readings for the Sixth Sunday of Easter

The common theme of John's Gospel which we are reading these weeks is the fact that Jesus, following his resurrection, has left us and returned to his Father. At the same time, he is still with us, but in a different way from before his death on the cross. And today's readings tell us that it is through the Spirit of the Father and the Son that that presence is experienced by us. We see this clearly expressed in today's Gospel passage. Jesus is telling his disciples at the Last Supper that, through the Spirit, he will continue to be with them—and us—forever. He calls the Spirit an 'Advocate'. In other biblical translations he is called a 'Counsellor' (NRSV, NIV), 'Comforter' (King James), Counsellor, Helper, Intercessor, Advocate, Strengthener, Standby (Amplified Bible), Advocate (NAB, NRSV). The Greek word is parakletos, from which comes the older word 'Paraclete'. Basically a 'paraclete' is someone like a defence lawyer, someone who stands by you in court and gives you support, advice and comfort in difficult situations where you need help. That is precisely the role of the Spirit in our Christian life. The Spirit teaches, guides, supports, consoles and comforts as we try to be faithful in our following of Christ's Way.

Queen of the May / Bring Flowers of the Rarest

This very popular hymn was composed by Mary E. Walsh, an American musician. It was first published in 1871. It is about spring, flowers and blossoms, in the pastoral setting of woodland, hillside and dale - and above all it's about the Queen of the May.

O Mary, we crown thee with blossoms today

Queen of the Angels and Queen of the May.

Depicting Mary wearing a crown is found in the 5th century, East and West. Later images have Mary seated on a throne, dressed like a queen and surrounded by angels and saints.

Christ is sometimes seen placing a crown on her head. This hymn has been recorded by many artists and perhaps most associated with Canon Sydney McEwan. He was born in Glasgow in 1908 into a poor family, went to Glasgow University and began a singing career. He went to the Scots College in Rome and was ordained in 1944. His concerts and records went to charity. He died on 26th September 1991 as parish priest of Dunoon, Argyll, in Western Scotland.

## Reflection

This Sunday, we are reminded that God’s love is always active and present in our lives. The coming of the Holy Spirit gives us strength. We are encouraged to trust that God is with us in every moment. Building on this, the "Good News" is reaching new people, and the Spirit is moving among them.

The psalm invites everyone to praise God for His mighty works. Peter calls us to speak of our hope with gentleness. In the Gospel, Jesus promises the Spirit will be with us forever. Jesus promises his disciples that He will ask the Father to send them the Advocate, the Holy Spirit, to be with them forever. If they love Him and keep His commandments, He also promises that He will come to them and reveal Himself to them, and whoever loves Him and keeps His word will be loved by the Father, and they will dwell with Him.

This message reminds us that God draws close to His people. It shows us how our faith grows when we live in love, speak with courage, and keep our eyes fixed on the Lord.

In Catholic devotion, May is traditionally associated with Mary as a special month of honour prayer, and love. The sources here do not explicitly name "May" as Mary's month, but they do show that Marian devotion is one of the Church's richest devotional traditions, expressed through the rosary, novenas, special prayers, litanies, pilgrimages, icons, and feast days. They also show that Marian devotion developed deeply in the Church from the New Testament, through the early Church and catacombs, into feast days, prayer books, and popular piety.

That makes the month of May fitting for Mary: it is a time when Catholics intensify exactly the kinds of devotions the Church already associates with her—especially the rosary and other contemplative prayers that lead believers more deeply into the heart of her Son. Mary is presented as Mother of God, Mother of the Church, and a central intercessor who comforts, protects, and leads believers toward Christ. So the spirit of May devotion is not to replace Jesus, but to honour Mary precisely because devotion to her 'will always lead us more deeply onto the heart of her son'
