Continuing t0 consider the Preface of the Mass [the prayer which introduced the Holy Holy and the Eucharistic Prayer], following the opening dialogue; which we have already considered, we enter into the substance of the Preface itself. The Preface begins with the following address to God the Father: It is truly right and just; our duty and our salvation; always and everywhere to give you thanks, Lord, holy Father, almighty and eternal God, through Christ our Lord. It takes up the final part of the introductory dialogue "It is right and just" [to lift up our hearts to the Lord] whereby the Celebrant takes up the acclamation of the people, joining themselves to the uprightness and holiness that belongs t0 God himself: The prayer continues with the affirmation that it is 'our duty and our salvation always and everywhere to give you thanks" This powerful statement of faith bears reflection. The act of thanksgiving is not, for the Christian; bland sentiment of appreciation: it is both duty and extraordinarily- our salvation! We have already considered how the very name of the Mass the Holy Eucharist means Thanksgiving, understood as Christ's own act of praise to God offered on the cross as an offering 'breathing out fragrance as he offered it to God' [cf: Ephesians 5:2]. It is in this sense that thanksgiving is a duty and our very salvation It is duty of the soul that believes and acknowledges God, and specifically acknowledges the full mystery of God revealed in Jesus Christ. For this reason it is also our salvation to acknowledge God's wonderful work in Jesus Christ. It is both an act of faith in Jesus Christ and joining of ourselves to his salvific action on the cross and in the resurrection; which is made manifest in the sacrifice of the Mass. Christ is acknowledged as the firstborn of all creation the eternal Son of the Father the one through whom all creation came to be and has been redeemed, the New Man, the Second Adam; the firstborn from the dead the one through whom all sin is forgiven and souls are made new, reborn in baptism to be sons and daughters of God the Father. The believing Christian is, therefore, bound to acknowledge Gods work in Jesus Christ the co-eternal Son, and to acknowledge all that he has made and re-made; to acknowledge his work in creation and his mission of love and redemption from the beginning to this very day and to the end of time Thanksgi ving therefore, is not a mere nod of the head nOr platitude expressed by familiar formulas: it is the very response of the soul seized by the mystery of Gods loving action from the beginning of creation through the work of redemption in Jesus Christ and His provident direction of all history and existence to his own all- consuming love. It is above all our part in Christ' s own self-offering to the Father.

THE SACRAMENT OF CONFIRMATION in the Catholic Church is a privileged moment of the outpouring of the Holy Spirit that deepens and increases the grace received at Baptism. Its primary effect is to give the confirmed a special strength of the Holy Spirit to spread and defend the faith by word and action, enabling them to be courageous witnesses of Christ and to never be ashamed of the Cross (CCC 1303) This sacrament completes the conferral of the universal priesthood of the faithful, authorising the confirmed to evangelise and officially witness their faith ("quasi Ex officio") (CCC 1305). Like Baptism, Confirmation imprints an indelible spiritual mark on the soul and thus may only be received once Who can receive Confirmation? Any baptised person who has reached the age of reason and spiritual maturity (typically around six or seven years old) may be confirmed. Proper instruction and preparation are required, which involve not only doctrinal understanding of the faith and sacraments but also fostering deeper relationship with Christ through prayer and attentiveness to the gifts of the Holy Spirit. Confirmation candidates should be in a state of grace, and it is recommended they receive the sacrament of Reconciliation beforehand. They also select sponsor ideally one of their baptismal sponsors, reinforcing the unity between Baptism and Confirmation. The ordinary minister of Confirmation in the Latin Rite is the bishop, signifying the close link between the sacrament's grace and the strengthening of the Church under the bishop's leadership. However, the bishop may delegate priest to confer Confirmation, especially during the Easter Vigil or other special occasions. In cases of danger of death, any priest may administer Confirmation to baptised but unconfirmed person. The rite itself consists of four parts: (1) candidates renew their baptismal promises and profess their faith; (2) a prayer invoking the Holy Spirit is prayed over the group; (3) the bishop lays hands on each candidate and anoints their forehead with chrism while saying, 'Be sealed with the Holy Spirit;' marking them as belonging entirely to Christ and enrolled in His service forever (CCC 1296); (4) a sign of peace concludes the ceremony, expressing ecclesial communion. Historically, Confirmation developed from the early Church's distinction between baptism by water and baptism by the Holy Spirit. Initially imparted together in the apostolic Church, the sacrament of Confirmation arose as infant Baptism became widespread, leading to a separation of the two rites. The bishop reserved the anointing and laying on of hands (Confirmation) for older baptised individuals capable of exercising the gifts of the Holy Spirit in strengthening and evangelising the Church community. The Eastern Church, however, continues to administer Baptism and Confirmation together, even to infants.
