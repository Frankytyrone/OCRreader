“My God, I believe, I adore, I hope and love You! I ask pardon of You for all those who do not believe, do not adore do not hope and do not love You!”

We have Jesus’ example of showing love through real relationships as our model, and it is up to us as the church to live out that example in our world.

This week let us be on the lookout for the outsiders in our community and these people who often get overlooked. Take the time to ask, “How are you doing?” Then listen to what they have to say. Jesus promises he knows our voice and we know His. As you show love through listening, do you hear the Shepherd’s voice speaking through other people? By listening and loving, relationships grow as we follow our good Shepherd.

**THE MASS – THE PREFACE**

Continuing to consider the Preface of the Mass (the prayer which introduced the Holy Holy and the Eucharistic Prayer), following the opening dialogue, which we have already considered, we enter into the substance of the Preface itself. The Preface begins with the following address to God the Father: It is truly right and just, our duty and our salvation, always and everywhere to give you thanks, Lord, holy Father, almighty and eternal God, through Christ our Lord. It takes up the final part of the introductory dialogue – “It is right and just” [to lift up our hearts to the Lord], whereby the Celebrant takes up the acclamation of the people, joining themselves to the uprightness and holiness that belongs to God himself. The prayer continues with the affirmation that it is “our duty and our salvation always and everywhere to give you thanks”. This powerful statement of faith bears reflection. The act of thanksgiving is not, for the Christian, a bland sentiment of appreciation: it is both a duty and – extraordinarily – our salvation! We have already considered how the very name of the Mass – the Holy Eucharist – means Thanksgiving, understood as Christ’s own act of praise to God offered on the cross as an offering ‘breathing out fragrance as he offered it to God’ [cf. Ephesians 5:2]. It is in this sense that thanksgiving is a duty and our very salvation. It is a duty of the soul that believes and acknowledges God, and specifically acknowledges the fully mystery of God revealed in Jesus Christ. For this reason it is also our salvation to acknowledge God’s wonderful work in Jesus Christ. It is both an act of faith in Jesus Christ and a joining of ourselves to his sacrificial action on the cross and in the resurrection, which is made manifest in the sacrifice of the Mass. Christ is acknowledged as the ‘firstborn of all creation’, the ‘eternal Son of the Father’, the one through whom all creation came to be and has been redeemed, the New Man, the Second Adam, the ‘firstborn from the dead’, the one through whom all sin is forgiven and souls are made new, reborn in baptism to be sons and daughters of God the Father. The believing Christian is, therefore, bound to acknowledge God’s work in Jesus Christ the co-eternal Son, and to acknowledge all that he has made and re-made; to acknowledge his work in creation and his mission of love and redemption from the beginning to this very day and the end of time. Thanksgiving, therefore, is not a mere ‘nod of the head’ nor a platitude expressed by familiar formulas: it is the very response of the soul inspired by the mystery of God’s loving action from the beginning of creation through the work of redemption in Jesus Christ and His provident direction of all history and existence to his own all-consuming love. It is above all our part in Christ’s own self-offering to the Father.

THE SACRAMENT OF CONFIRMATION in the Catholic

Church is a privileged opportunity for receiving the Holy

Spirit that deepens and increases the grace received at

Baptism. Its primary effect is to give the confirmed a special

strength of the Holy Spirit to spread and defend the faith in

word and action, enabling them to be courageous witnesses

of Christ and never to be ashamed of the Cross (CCC 1303).

This sacrament epitomizes the central role of the universal

priesthood of the faithful, authorizing the confirmed to

evangelise and actively fulfill their witness for their faith &

others (CCC 1305). Like Baptism, Confirmation imprints an

indelible spiritual mark on the soul and thus may only be

received once.

Who can receive Confirmation? Any baptised person who

has reached the age of reason and spiritual maturity

(typically around six or seven years old) may be confirmed.

Proper instruction and preparation are required, which

involve not only doctrinal understanding of the faith and

sacraments but also fostering a deeper relationship with

Christ through prayer and attentiveness to the gifts of the

Holy Spirit. Confirmation candidates should be in a state of

grace, and it is recommended they receive the sacrament of

Reconciliation beforehand. They also select a sponsor,

usually one of the baptismal sponsors, reinforcing the unity

between Baptism and Confirmation.

The ordinary minister of Confirmation in the Latin Rite is the

bishop, signifying the close link between the sacraments of

grace and the strengthening of the Church under the

bishop’s leadership. However, the bishop may delegate a

priest to confer Confirmation, especially during the Easter

Vigil or other special occasions. In cases of danger of death,

any priest may administer Confirmation to a baptised but

unconfirmed person.

The rite itself consists of four parts: (1) candidates renew

their baptismal promises and profess their faith; (2) a prayer

is offered for the coming of the Holy Spirit on the group; (3) the

bishop lays hands on each candidate and anoints their

forehead while reciting, “Be sealed with the Gift of the

Holy Spirit;” (4) the congregation joins prayers and the

confirmed in His service forever (CCC 1289); (4.a) is offered

words marking ceremony, expressing the separation and

the Church’s full assistance. The distinction between the

theology of Confirmation is to deepen the unity and baptism

spirituality, which may often be lacking and separating

Church. The Sacrament of Confirmation arose as infant

Baptism became widespread, leading to an integration of

two rites. The sacramental rite for Confirmation is the laying

of hands (Confirmation) for older baptised individuals

provided exercising the gift of the Holy Spirit and

ministering or exemplifying commitment as community. The

Eastern Church, however, continues to administer Baptism

and Confirmation together, often for infants.
